
---

> At 3+ years, ~20–30% of an interview loop is non-technical: self intro, project walkthrough, behavioral (managerial), and HR. Product-based companies weight this heavily — especially "tell me about yourself" and deep project follow-ups.

---

## 1. Self Introduction ("Tell me about yourself")

- 🟢 Have a 60–90 second structured intro ready.
- 🟢 Structure: **current role → tech stack → main project → what you're looking for next**.
- 🟡 Tailor the tech emphasis to the role (Java-heavy? lead with Spring Boot. Full stack? mention React/Node).
- 🟡 End with a forward-looking line — why this company/role interests you.
- 💡 Don't recite your resume. Hit the 3 things you want them to remember.

**Common follow-ups:**
- 💡 Walk me through your journey so far.
- 💡 What's the most impactful thing you've shipped?
- 💡 Why did you choose backend / full stack as your focus?

---

## 2. Project Walkthrough (Asked in Every Round)

**The big ones:**
- 💡 Walk me through your current project architecture.
- 💡 What is the business domain? Who is the client / end user?
- 💡 What are your day-to-day responsibilities?
- 💡 What's your team size? What's your role on the team?
- 💡 What features have you built end-to-end?
- 💡 What technologies and tools are you using? (be ready for 2–3 follow-ups on each)
- 💡 How does your team follow Agile? Explain your sprint process.
- 💡 How do you handle code reviews?
- 💡 How do you deploy? Walk through the CI/CD pipeline.
- 💡 How does the frontend communicate with the backend?
- 💡 What database are you using and why was it chosen?
- 💡 How do you handle dev / staging / prod configs?

**Deep follow-ups (game changers):**
- 💡 What was the most challenging problem you solved? How did you approach it?
- 💡 How did you improve performance? (be ready with numbers)
- 💡 How does your system handle high traffic / scale?
- 💡 What happens if a dependent service goes down?
- 💡 How did you secure your APIs?
- 💡 How do you roll back a failed deployment?
- 💡 How did you handle a DB schema change / migration without downtime?
- 💡 What architectural decisions did you make? Why?
- 💡 What would you redesign if you started over?

## 3. Behavioral / Managerial (STAR)

**The STAR framework — use it for every behavioral answer:**

- **S — Situation**: context. 1–2 sentences. What was happening, who was involved.
- **T — Task**: your specific responsibility. What did _you_ need to do?
- **A — Action**: what you actually did. Use "I", not "we". Be specific.
- **R — Result**: outcome. Quantify when possible (latency dropped X%, deploy time halved, zero bugs in prod).

> Prep 4–5 "hero stories" you can reshape to fit different questions (ownership, conflict, failure, scope change, tight deadline). One story should map to 2–3 questions.

**Ownership & Drive:**
- 💡 Tell me about a time you took ownership of a feature end-to-end.
- 💡 Tell me about a time you went beyond your assigned scope.

**Collaboration & Conflict:**
- 💡 How do you handle disagreements with teammates?
- 💡 Tell me about a time you disagreed with your tech lead / manager. How did it resolve?

**Delivery Under Pressure:**
- 💡 Describe a time you worked under a tight deadline.
- 💡 Tell me about a time requirements changed mid-sprint — how did you adapt?

**Failure & Learning:**
- 💡 Tell me about a bug you shipped to production. What happened and what did you learn?
- 💡 Describe a technical decision you regret. What would you do differently?

**Quality & Process:**
- 💡 How do you ensure code quality in your work?
- 💡 What do you do when you're stuck on a problem for more than an hour?

## 4. HR Round

- 🟢 Tell me about yourself. (same as Section 1, but HR often wants it shorter — ~60 seconds)
- 🟢 Why are you looking for a change?
- 🟢 Why this company?
- 🟢 Where do you see yourself in 3–5 years?
- 🟢 What is your current CTC? What are your expectations?
- 🟢 What is your notice period? Can it be negotiated?
- 🟢 Are you open to the work location / hybrid model?
- 🟢 Do you have any offers in hand? (answer honestly but don't over-share)
- 🟢 Do you have any questions for us?

**Tips:**
- Never badmouth your current/previous employer. Frame it as "looking for bigger scope / deeper challenge / product ownership", not "my current place is bad."
- For salary: research bands for the role/level/location before the call. Give a range, not a single number, and tie it to market + your experience.
- For "why this company": 2–3 specific reasons — product, tech stack, scale, culture, learning path. Not generic "big brand" lines.
- For 3–5 years: frame as technical depth + ownership growth (e.g., tech lead, staff engineer, architect), not job title chasing.

---

## 5. Questions You Should Ask the Interviewer

> Always have 3–4 ready. Not asking any signals low interest.

**About the role:**
- What are the biggest technical challenges the team is tackling right now?
- What does success look like in the first 6 months?

**About the tech:**
- What's the tech stack like? How is technical debt prioritized?

**About growth:**
- How does the team handle knowledge sharing and mentorship?

**About culture:**
- What's the on-call / incident response model like?

---

## 6. Interview Strategy Reminders

- **Short answer first**, then expand if asked. Don't monologue.
- **Say "in my project…"** 2–3 times per round — it grounds every answer in real experience.
- **Don't bluff.** "I haven't used that directly, but my understanding is…" is always better than making something up. Interviewers probe claims.
- **Ask clarifying questions** on ambiguous prompts — especially system design and coding. Interviewers explicitly look for this.
- **Quantify results** when you can: "reduced API latency from 800ms to 200ms", "cut deploy time in half", not "made it faster".
- **Stay calm during live coding.** Talk through your approach before writing. Narrate what you're doing and why.
- **Know your resume cold.** For every tech you list, expect 2–3 follow-ups. If you can't explain it past buzzword level, remove it from your resume.
